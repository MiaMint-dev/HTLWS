Back Home

After tonight, who knows when I’ll be able to wear an oversized T-shirt with no pants, no bra, and flap around in the living room? By flapping, I meant dancing—I wasn’t sure I could call it that. Also, I have a mint chocolate ice cream tub wrapped in my arms. And with the spoon, I lead the orchestra of the live version of Call Out My Name. Such a dramatic night. The greatest of the past days where I sought to live like no tomorrow. All in hopes I could ignore the grim future creeping over me like a rain cloud. Countless times I woke up wondering if I’ve been dreaming all this time. And the disappointment of an empty house assured me I was conscious through it all. You could say that I’ve been processing until the very last day, in which I decided to go all in.
Tonight’s all I’ve got—for a while. I drop onto the couch, the idea that all I’ve got is so…temporary had drained the last stretch of my sugar rush. I know I’ll regret not enjoying my last bit of freedom, but at the same time, how could I enjoy a moment I know will vanish after a night’s sleep? The song faded in my ears as my thoughts grew louder.
What a burden they gave me. When these strangers fill every room of the house, I fear I’ll reach the pinnacle of isolation. I was always alone, but at least I was free. This is insanity. It never made sense, and I fear it never will. They put their feelings first then failed to consider mine. Now my comfort—what I value most—is confined to my bedroom. I might uncover a few limits there too. No more loud music, for one.
My inner monologue was curtly interrupted by the door bell. It only rang once. I sprung up to pause my music which had switched to the Weeknd’s earliest work, House Of Balloons—can’t be caught listening to that by a stranger at the door. That stranger must be a delivery driver. My laptop stand was supposed to arrive today, and it did so later than expected. I wiped my hands damp from holding the ice cream tub on my grey T-shirt. The stain is grand, but it’ll dry soon and delivery drivers don’t linger at the door anyway. I don’t bother to turn on the porch light and twist the knob. My eyes were on the ground, looking for a box, instead I saw black sneakers.
I turned on the porch light right away. I was afraid but I raised my head. This was no delivery driver, at least not one in uniform. The stranger smiled at me so kindly it was slightly unsettling. I then took notice of his fluffy head of honey brown hair while attempting to evade his eyes. The waves were so plentiful it made them look like messy curls.
“Hi, Rain?” he uttered. “I’m Hael.”
His name was a blow I don’t think I could ever recover from. It was a drop from the rain cloud I mentioned earlier. One for when I realized who he was. Another for when I realized what filled his field of vision. A stained T-shirt. Bare feet. Messy hair from too much “dancing”. Then the drops followed each other. The forecast was wrong.
“I can explain,” I first said, sounding ten times more calm than I felt.
“No, no. I should be the one explaining,” he said, his smile gradually fading. “My flight was changed to an earlier one and my phone was dead. I couldn’t tell my father and your mother so she’d warn you. I even thought of booking a hotel for the night so I’d show up in the morning as expected, but I ran into further difficulties there,” his rambles were hurried while his demeanour was composed. His words rang far from excuses. “I’m so sorry,” he continued.
If truth be told, there’s nothing he needs to apologize for. Not him. But I’m in no position to pity him. The wind tugged at the hem of my shirt, it’s a chilly night. We’re in September after all and this night might just be the coldest of the month. My arms and legs are covered in goosebumps, his arms are too. “You should come in,” I said, moving away from the door giving him the space to step in. There’s no reason to leave him standing there. He’s a stranger yes, but one I expected.
He kept his gaze up; it never drifted down my bare legs. For that, I was relieved. He wouldn’t look past my face. The only downside is the frequent eye contact that it caused. As if I weren’t embarrassed enough. He dragged a black suitcase in. The luggage was saturated with illustrated stickers. The stickers were far from generic. No smileys, fire emblems, or Eiffel Tower. Only a variety of characters from I don’t know where. A comic, maybe?
“Again. I apologize. I really took you by surprise,” he said, sweeping his hair behind. His forehead uncovered, I could see streaks of sweat. Right where strands of hair were a little darker than others. So they weren’t dyed. He must’ve been quite nervous to be sweating despite the cold weather.
“It’s alright. There was nothing you could do,” I say matter-of-factly rather than kindly—at least that’s how I’d like to sound.
I’ve never been a host. I don’t know how to be a host. I did not want to be the host. Yet, the first day’s here—without a warning.
“I can show you to your room,” I said, motioning to the stairs.
The sooner I fulfill my initial duties, the sooner I can repatriate to my cocoon. There’s no need to socialize. That one’s not written. Besides, I won’t allow myself to, not dressed like this. Correction; barely dressed. The plan is to call it a night and have a proper first meeting when the sun’s up just like my pants.
“I’ll follow wherever. Thank you,” he said, smiling with eyes that creased like lemon slices. He lowers the handle of his suitcase and lifts it promptly.
“It’s not that far,” I said with a chuckle. It just escaped out of me, like that. It had no right to.
Could this be any more awkward? Just bury me already and don’t let me attempt humour ever again.

What a first impression I’ve made. She paled the second I mentioned my name. Still, she let me in without questions, or even complaints—I deserved those. I ruined her one last comfortable night, it seems. I should’ve tried harder to find accommodations, or perhaps have spent the night at the airport. Plenty do that for connecting flights. You’re truly not yourself after a long flight. Reaching the ground anew, all I dreamed of was a comfortable bed.
Before taking the lead on the stairs, Rain proposed to carry my suitcase. I refused. Then she tried asking for my backpack instead. She was met with the same answer. How could I accept when I already feel so guilty for barging in early? It doesn’t take a genius to see that I interrupted someone’s last taste of freedom—for a while. I took it away from right under her nose. She had opened the door so unassuming.
“You can pick a room, if you’d like. It can either be the one on the right, or far left.”
She stood in front of the towel closet. I’d have mistaken it for a room, were it not left ajar.
“I’ll take any room, but since I have the choice, I’ll pick the one near the stairs,” it was the closest on the right.
She nodded then opened the door for me.
“Seems you made the lucky choice. This was my parents’ room, it’s pretty large.”
My eyes widened and the corners of my mouth wobbled in an attempt to form a smile. The space is too large for a single person to use. It’s wasted on me. The sheer size of it reminds me of my first studio. The house looked humble and familial from outside. The dining room and living room, which I caught a glimpse of, seemed common too. I guess you could expect something this grand from the primary bedroom. It sure lived up to its name. How small must the two other rooms be?
My rent is already discounted, I really shouldn’t be taking the largest room. It’s basically an apartment. There’s a large bed, a spacious desk area far enough from the bed, a wall of mirrors—behind them, immense closet space—and even a round table with three chairs. The bathroom is right next door too. Albeit that could be a curse if the walls are thin. I stepped out of the room.
“You should be using the room or at least renting it for a higher price. It would only be fair,” I said.
She stared at me, her eyes rounding.
“No, no, no,” she was quick to say with enthusiasm. “This is definitely your room. You chose it. And I’m satisfied with my room, even if I weren’t, moving is a hassle.”
She hauled my suitcase back in the room, a smile showing up for the first time. “Make yourself at home. It is your home, now,” she said.
I could tell she meant it, but my guilt stayed firm like it was glued with precision.
“Would you like something to drink, or eat? I can bring it upstairs so you can rest from your trip,” she just had to say. The guilt grew heavier then.
“I’m alright, thank you. Thank you so much.”
I didn’t worry too much about who I’d be living with, seeing I’m in no position to be selective, but I’ll admit that I’m relieved. Rain appears to be genuinely kind. What I’ve heard of her wasn’t sugar coated. It didn’t come from the biased view of a mother. Seems my days here will be just fine.
